# U.S. Farm Equipment Capital Stock: Age, Depreciation, and Investment Gap

## The Core Finding

**The average American farm tractor is over 25 years old.** The recommended economic life is 15 years. The fleet is operating 10+ years past optimal replacement, and it's getting worse.

## 6. The Capital Replacement Gap

### Expenditure-to-Consumption Ratio

This ratio measures whether new equipment spending exceeds depreciation (>1.0 = fleet renewal, <1.0 = fleet aging).

| Period | Ratio | Interpretation |
|--------|-------|----------------|
| 1973-2022 average | 1.048 | Barely above breakeven over 50 years |
| 1979 (peak) | High | 1970s investment boom |
| **1986** | **0.52** | Farm crisis -- only replacing half of what wore out |
| 2008-2011 | 1.70+ | Commodity boom, massive fleet renewal |
| 2014 | 1.70 | Peak spending ($50.3B in 2021$) |
| **2014-2018** | **<0.70** | Approaching underinvestment again |
| 2022 | 1.70 | Robust replacement |
| **2024-2025** | **Declining sharply** | Sales down 15-20%, heading toward underinvestment |

**The pattern is unmistakable**: investment closely tracks commodity prices and net farm income. Farmers defer equipment purchases during downturns, creating a capital replacement gap that compounds over time.

## 8. The Right-to-Repair Factor

### How It Accelerates Fleet Aging

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Annual farmer losses from tractor downtime | $3 billion |
| Annual excess repair costs (dealer-only) | $1.2 billion |
| Total potential savings from Right to Repair | $4.2 billion/year |
| Farmers who lost crops due to equipment breakdown | 53% (PIRG survey) |
| Farmers who bought older equipment to avoid software locks | 77% (surveyed) |
| Independent repair cost vs. dealer | ~50% less, ~50% faster |

**Two paradoxical effects:**
1. Some farmers deliberately buy pre-software tractors (1980s-1990s) they can repair themselves, keeping 30-40 year old machines in service. A 1980 John Deere 4440 sold for $43,500 in April 2019.
2. Other farmers extend the life of aging equipment because the total cost of ownership for new, dealer-dependent machines is unfavorable at current financing rates.

**FTC sued John Deere in January 2025** over repair restrictions. Colorado enacted the first farm equipment Right to Repair law in April 2023. AFBF negotiated MOUs with Deere, CNH, AGCO, Kubota, and CLAAS covering ~75% of equipment sold.

## 9. Tax Policy: Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation

Tax treatment is a major driver of equipment investment timing:

| Period | Bonus Depreciation |
|--------|-------------------|
| Through 2022 | 100% first-year expensing |
| 2023 | 80% |
| 2024 | 60% |
| 2025 (pre-OBBBA) | 40% (scheduled phase-down) |
| **July 2025 (OBBBA enacted)** | **100% restored** |
| Section 179 limit (2025) | Raised to $2.5 million |
| Section 179 limit (2026) | $2.56 million |

Without bonus depreciation, farms faced average annual tax increases of ~$87,000/year. The restoration may stimulate some equipment investment in late 2025/2026, but with financing at 8% and farm income declining, the effect may be muted.

## 10. Building a Stock-and-Flow Model

### Framework

```
Net Fleet Change = New Sales - Exports - Retirements/Scrappage

Scrappage (2017-2022) = Stock_2017 + Cumulative_Sales(2018-2022)
                        - Cumulative_Exports(2018-2022) - Stock_2022
```

### Data Sources for the Model

| Data Need | Source | Cost |
|-----------|--------|------|
| Annual new sales by HP class | AEM monthly reports | Free (recent) / Paid (40-year history) |
| Total fleet on farms (every 5 yr) | USDA Census of Ag, Table 45 | Free (NASS Quick Stats) |
| Import/export trade flows | USITC DataWeb (HS 8701, 8433) | Free |
| Depreciation curves | Iowa State Extension (A3-29) | Free |
| Manufacturer inventories | FRED Series A33ATI | Free |
| Used equipment auction values | Sandhills / Tractor Zoom | Free (some) / Paid |
| Farm sector balance sheet | USDA ERS | Free |
| Capital depreciation time series | FRED Series B1449C1A027NBEA | Free |
| BEA net stock by asset type | BEA Fixed Assets Tables | Free |
| Farm credit conditions | KC Fed, Chicago Fed, Minneapolis Fed | Free |

### Rough Flow Estimate (2024)

| Flow | Units |
|------|-------|
| **Inflows**: New retail sales | ~217,000 tractors |
| **Outflows**: Exports | ~37,000 tractors |
| **Outflows**: Estimated retirements | ~150,000-200,000 tractors |
| **Net change** | Roughly flat to slight decline |

During the 2020-2021 boom, inflows significantly exceeded outflows. In the current downturn, the fleet is likely shrinking or at best stable, while aging continues.

## 11. Summary: The Investment Gap

**The American farm equipment fleet is massively aged and under-capitalized:**

1. At 25+ years average age vs. 15 years economic life, the typical tractor is 67% past optimal replacement
2. Only 10-16% of the fleet is less than 5 years old
3. The expenditure-to-consumption ratio repeatedly drops below 1.0 during downturns, meaning the fleet depreciates faster than it is replaced
4. The current downturn (sales -15-20%, financing at 8%, farm income falling) is compounding the problem
5. Right-to-repair restrictions paradoxically incentivize keeping older equipment, adding $4.2B/year in costs
6. Equipment prices have more than doubled since 1997 (PPI: 139.5 → 290.2), making replacement increasingly expensive
7. The total capital stock of ~$380-420 billion in machinery sits on a $4.54 trillion farm asset base, but its effective productive capacity is deeply impaired by age

**Back-of-envelope investment gap**: If the "right" level of equipment spending sustains a 15-year replacement cycle on a ~$400 billion stock, that's ~$27 billion/year just in depreciation. Actual capital consumption runs $31-33 billion. With sales now falling to levels that don't cover depreciation, the gap widens with each passing year. The last time this persisted (1982-1986), the farm crisis resulted in widespread farm failures and a fundamental restructuring of American agriculture.
