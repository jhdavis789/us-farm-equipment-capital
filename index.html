The Investment Gap

Gross investment in agricultural machinery vs. capital consumption (depreciation), 1970–2025. When investment falls below depreciation, the fleet is shrinking in real terms.

Gross Investment vs. Depreciation

Source: BEA via FRED — C268RC1A027NBEA (investment) and BOGZ1FA136330023Q (depreciation, annualized from quarterly SAAR)

Investment-to-Depreciation Ratio

Ratio > 1.0 = fleet renewal. Ratio < 1.0 = fleet shrinking. The 1.0 line is the breakeven threshold.

Cumulative Net Investment Since 1970

Running total of (investment - depreciation). Negative = accumulated underinvestment.
Key finding: The U.S. farm equipment fleet has been in net decline since 2023. The 2025 investment-to-depreciation ratio of 0.73x matches the worst levels of the 2016–2017 downturn. At current rates, farmers are replacing only 73 cents of equipment for every dollar that wears out. The cumulative effect over decades of cyclical underinvestment has left the fleet far older than economically optimal.

Fleet Age

How old is the equipment? The Census only tracks two buckets: under 5 years and 5+ years. We derive the implied replacement cycle from Census stock data and AEM annual sales.

Share of Fleet Under 5 Years Old

Source: USDA Census of Agriculture. Only ~12% of the fleet is "new" in any given Census year.

Implied Replacement Cycle by HP Class

Census fleet ÷ AEM annual sales = years to fully replace fleet at current sales rate. Red dashed line = 15-year recommended economic life.

Estimated Replacement Cycle Over Time (by HP Class)

Using 2022 Census fleet counts and AEM annual sales for each year. Shows how the replacement cycle expands during downturns.
The serious farming equipment is barely turning over. At 2024 sales rates, it would take 46 years to replace the 100+ HP tractor fleet and 54 years to replace combines. Even at the 2021 boom peak, large tractors were on a 36-year replacement cycle — 2.4x their recommended 15-year economic life. The <40 HP segment (mostly hobby/lifestyle tractors) turns over quickly, masking the severity of underinvestment in the equipment that actually feeds the country.

Price inflation is masking the real decline. In nominal terms, the capital stock has risen from $270B (2017) to $359B (2025). But real investment peaked at $48.7B in 2014 and has fallen to $28.2B in 2025 — a 42% decline in actual equipment volumes. Equipment prices have risen 29% since 2017 alone (and 108% since 1997), meaning farmers are paying much more for each unit of productive capacity.

Data Sources & Methodology

  • Investment (Gross): BEA/FRED series C268RC1A027NBEA — Private fixed investment in agricultural machinery, nominal, annual 1929–2025. FRED
  • Investment (Real): BEA/FRED series C268RX1A020NBEA — Real private fixed investment in agricultural machinery, 2017 dollars, 2007–2025. FRED
  • Depreciation: BEA/FRED series BOGZ1FA136330023Q — Farm business consumption of fixed capital, nonresidential equipment, quarterly SAAR, 1946–2025. Annual values = average of 4 quarters. FRED
  • Net Capital Stock: BEA/FRED series BOGZ1FL135013265Q — Farm business nonresidential equipment, current-cost net stock, quarterly, 1945–2025. FRED
  • Price Index: BEA/FRED series B268RG3A086NBEA — Agricultural machinery price index, 2017=100, 1929–2025. FRED
  • Census Fleet Data: USDA Census of Agriculture, Table 45 — Selected Machinery and Equipment on Operation. Tractors by HP class, age, combines, etc. NASS / Quick Stats
  • Census Machinery Values: USDA Census of Agriculture, Table 44 — Value of Machinery and Equipment on Operation. NASS
  • Trade Data (2000-2009): UN Comtrade API — HS 8701 (tractors), HS 8433 (harvesting/threshing machinery). Comtrade
  • Trade Data (2010-2024): U.S. Census Bureau International Trade API — HS 8701, HS 8433, HS10-level detail. USITC DataWeb
  • AEM Sales: Association of Equipment Manufacturers monthly/annual retail sales reports. AEM
  • Equipment Life Recommendations: Iowa State Extension, Ag Decision Maker A3-29 — 15 years for tractors, 10–12 years for combines. ISU
  • Fleet Age Estimate (25–27 years): Myers et al. (2010), "Tractors and Rollover Protection in the United States," J. Agromedicine 15(3). PubMed

Methodology notes: The investment-to-depreciation ratio is computed as gross nominal investment / annualized capital consumption (average of 4 quarterly SAAR values). Both series are from BEA national accounts and use the same price basis, making the ratio meaningful. "Implied replacement cycle" = Census stock / AEM annual retail sales. This overstates the cycle slightly because some sold units replace retired ones (not counted by Census) and some sales go to non-farm buyers (especially <40 HP). For 100+ HP equipment, virtually all sales are to farms, so the metric is most reliable for that class.
